Mini Footprints 

SaaS-Based E-Commerce Store

Built by: Mamata Pokharel | BSc.CSIT, St. Xavier's College, Kathmandu | Course: E-Commerce | Platform: Blanxer (SaaS e-commerce CMS)

Status: Built and fully operational during the platform's free trial period; the store is no longer hosted. 
Screenshots of the storefront, admin panel, and analytics dashboards are included in /screenshots.

Overview:

Mini Footprints is a complete e-commerce store for baby party shoes, built end to end from market research and business modelling-through to storefront build, payment integration, search optimisation, and analytics reporting.


The niche was chosen deliberately : parents of children aged 0–5 shopping for footwear for birthdays, weddings, and festive occasions. Narrow intent, high emotional value, low competition. A Business Model Canvas came first (customer segments, value proposition, channels, revenue streams), and every downstream decision   category naming, product copy, meta descriptions was made to serve that specific searcher.

Search & discoverability

This is the part of the project I care about most, and the reason a store nobody can find is because it is just a website. So, for this we need to implement the following techniques so that it's visibility and discoverability ranks higher whenever they are searched on any browsing platform.

i. On-page optimisation

•	Page-level meta titles and meta descriptions written for every page, product, and category

•	Product copy structured for both readers and crawlers: descriptive H1s, feature lists, sizing tables, and use-case sections ("Perfect for: birthday parties, weddings, photoshoots") that target the language a parent would actually search with

•	Image optimisation and consistent naming across the media library


ii. Site architecture

•	Category taxonomy (Baby Boy / Baby Girl / Unisex) designed as a crawlable, searchable hierarchy rather than a flat product dump

•	Clean internal linking between home → category → product → cart, so both users and crawlers reach any product in a small number of clicks

•	Supporting static pages (About, Contact, Shipping, Refund, Privacy) to establish the trust signals search engines and shoppers both look for

iii. Intent research

•	Product naming and descriptions built around occasion-based intent (party shoes, wedding, first birthday) rather than generic terms like "baby shoes," on the reasoning that a narrow, specific query is easier to rank for and converts far better




Measurement & reporting

i.	Google Analytics 4 configured from scratch and connected to the live store

ii.	Google Tag Manager installed for centralised tag deployment without editing site code

iii.	Tracked and read: traffic sources, user acquisition, landing page performance, engagement time, retention cohorts, and new vs. returning users

iv.	Used the reports to understand where visitors came from and where they dropped off   not just how many there were


What I built

a.	Storefront: custom-branded homepage, category architecture (Baby Boy / Baby Girl / Unisex), product listing and detail pages, filters, cart, and checkout

b.	Product catalogue: listings with descriptions, sizing guides, media, and discount pricing

c.	Payments: eSewa, Khalti, and Cash on Delivery integration with automated order confirmation

d.	SEO: page-level meta titles, meta descriptions, image optimisation, and a searchable category structure to improve organic discoverability

e.	Analytics: Google Analytics 4 and Google Tag Manager configured to track traffic sources, user acquisition, retention, engagement, and new vs. returning users

f.	Customer engagement: Tawk.to live chat plugin and automated SMS notifications for each order status


What I'd do next

Being honest about the gaps, because knowing and acknowledging them is necessary too:

i.	Keyword research with real data   this build relied on reasoned assumptions about search intent, not volume data. Search Console and a keyword tool would replace guesswork with evidence.

ii.	Technical audit   crawl the site, check index coverage, page speed, mobile usability, and structured data (a Product schema with price and availability would be the obvious first win for rich results).

iii.	Content strategy   a blog targeting informational queries ("how to measure a baby's shoe size") to capture traffic further up the funnel and build topical authority.

iv.	Off-page   outreach to Nepali parenting blogs and local directories to build the authority signals a new domain has none of.


Key takeaway

Building the store taught me the mechanics. Watching the analytics afterwards taught me the actual lesson: traffic is not an accident, and the difference between a store that gets found and one that doesn't comes down to decisions made in the metadata, the site structure, and the language you choose to describe what you sell.
